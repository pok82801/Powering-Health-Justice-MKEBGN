The Burden at a Glance

Medical debt is a growing crisis across the United States, affecting millions of individuals and households. Nationally, about 1 in 12 adults (roughly 8%) carry medical debt. However, Wisconsin exceeds this national trend in several key ways.

Uncompensated Care Breakdown

In 2023, Wisconsin hospitals reported approximately $1.36 billion in uncompensated care — care provided without payment:

  • $731 million was bad debt — bills patients could not pay
  • $627 million was charity care — free or reduced-cost care for eligible patients
Over recent years, charity care has decreased while bad debt has increased, suggesting that more individuals are falling through eligibility gaps and are unable to access financial assistance.

Milwaukee: Ground Zero

Hospitals in Milwaukee carry a disproportionate share of Wisconsin's medical debt burden:

  • $189 million in charity care in 2022 — about half of the state total
  • $160 million in bad debt — indicating that even in urban areas with more healthcare infrastructure, affordability remains a major issue

Broader Impacts of Medical Debt

Medical debt extends far beyond healthcare costs. It can:

  • Lower credit scores
  • Make it harder to secure housing or employment
  • Contribute to long-term financial stress and instability

What's Being Done?

The policy landscape in Wisconsin

Current Policy & Legal Landscape

The table below summarizes Wisconsin's current policies relating to medical debt. While some protections exist, significant gaps remain.

Policy Area Description Status
Debt Collection Laws Wisconsin and federal laws (FDCPA, Wis. Consumer Act Ch. 421-427) prohibit harassment and deceptive collection practices. However, they do not prevent medical debt from being reported to credit bureaus. In effect
Consumer Help Resources State agencies provide guidance on negotiating bills, payment plans, and credit counseling. These supports are informational and voluntary — they do not guarantee relief. Available
Worker's Comp Fee Schedule Wis. Stat. § 102.42 limits what providers can charge for work-related injuries. These protections apply only to workplace injuries. Enacted
Oversight & Assistance Wisconsin lacks stronger protections around hospital billing transparency and debt collection. Compared to other states, oversight remains limited. Needs improvement

Recent & Proposed Legislation

Wisconsin currently does not have specific laws that limit how hospitals and debt collectors handle medical debt for most patients. A recent policy in the 2025 state budget establishes a worker's compensation hospital fee schedule, creating standardized payment caps for hospital services related to workplace injuries.

However, the scope is limited: It applies only to employees receiving care for work-related injuries under worker's compensation, excluding uninsured individuals, privately insured patients, and anyone with medical debt from non-work-related conditions.

Media & Public Discourse

From 2022 to early 2025, media coverage of medical debt in Wisconsin has remained consistent and increasingly urgent, highlighting rising debt levels, gaps in financial assistance, and growing policy attention.

November 2022
Declining charity care, rising debt

A Wisconsin Examiner report found that hospital charity care was declining while medical debt was increasing across the state, suggesting fewer patients are receiving financial assistance even as the need grows.

January 2023
Milwaukee County debt relief proposal

Urban Milwaukee reported on a proposal for Milwaukee County to eliminate up to $153 million in medical debt by partnering with organizations that purchase and forgive debt at a fraction of its value — targeting low-income residents and communities of color.

October 2024
Advocacy group criticism

ABC for Health criticized Milwaukee hospitals for not doing enough to help low-income patients avoid medical debt, emphasizing gaps in financial assistance policies.

November 2024
Rising debt post-pandemic

Wisconsin Public Radio reported that unpaid medical bills were rising again, in part due to the rollback of pandemic-era protections, including the loss of Medicaid coverage for many residents.

January 2025
Hospital reform efforts

The Guardian examined efforts by Advocate Health to scale back aggressive debt collection practices. While these reforms signal progress, many patients reported that changes do not go far enough.
